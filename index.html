If DCYF issued a finding against you, you may have rights they never told you about.

Understanding Your Letter

What is an "Unfounded with Reasonable Concern" finding?

When DCYF investigates a family and does not find abuse or neglect, they sometimes close the case with a designation called "Unfounded with Reasonable Concern" — often abbreviated URC. On its face, it sounds like a clearance. In practice, it is something more complicated.

What a URC finding typically says

The investigation found no evidence of abuse or neglect. However, DCYF identified concerns that were noted in the record. The case is being closed.

What it does not say: what those concerns were, whether they are accurate, what standard was used to evaluate them, or that you had the right to challenge them.

Until a landmark ruling in September 2025, DCYF took the position that URC findings were final and unreviewable — meaning families had no formal way to dispute them, correct errors, or clear their record. Thousands of families were told, in effect: the case is closed, there is nothing to appeal, move on.

That position has now been rejected by an Administrative Law Judge. The ruling found that URC findings are subject to administrative appeal under New Hampshire law — a ruling with consequences for every family that received one.

Three Things to Know

What the ruling means — in plain terms

1

You may have appeal rights DCYF never disclosed

If you received a URC letter and were told there was nothing to appeal, that advice may have been wrong. The September 2025 ruling directly contradicts DCYF's longstanding position.

2

Your finding may be legally defective

New Hampshire law requires administrative findings to include findings of fact and conclusions of law. URC letters were issued without either. And DCYF's own official forms had no place to put them — this wasn't a one-time mistake.

3

Time limits may apply — but there are arguments to extend them

Administrative appeals normally have deadlines. However, when an agency actively prevented families from appealing by telling them no appeal existed, there are legal arguments for accepting late filings. This is being actively litigated.

The September 25, 2025 Ruling

What an ALJ decided — and why it matters

DCYF's motion to dismiss the administrative appeal was denied. The Administrative Appeals Unit held that Unfounded with Reasonable Concern findings are subject to administrative appeal under RSA 541-A — a category the agency had maintained was entirely unreviewable.

ALJ Zachary Towle — AAU Docket 2025-1149 — September 25, 2025

This ruling came in a specific case — but its logic applies broadly. DCYF's argument for dismissal rested on the claim that URC findings aren't "decisions" in a legal sense. The ALJ rejected that argument. If DCYF cannot use that defense in this case, it cannot use it in yours. Three administrative appeals are now pending before the AAU alongside a parallel federal civil rights case.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

Does this mean DCYF did something wrong in my case?

Not necessarily. "Unfounded with Reasonable Concern" means DCYF did not find abuse or neglect. The legal issue here is procedural: whether the way these findings were issued — without findings of fact, conclusions of law, or appeal notice — complies with state administrative law requirements.

A procedurally defective finding is a separate question from whether the underlying concerns were valid. You may have grounds to challenge the finding even if some of DCYF's concerns had a factual basis.

Beyond individual cases, DCYF's official forms for URC findings were themselves missing the required elements — a systemic issue that affected thousands of families, not just yours.

Is it too late to appeal? My letter is from 2021 or 2022.

Possibly not — there are legal arguments for accepting late filings when an agency actively prevented families from appealing by telling them no appeal right existed. This is unresolved and actively being litigated. The Your Rights page covers this in detail, including the specific legal arguments and what they depend on.

Do I need a lawyer to appeal?

No — AAU appeals can be filed pro se. That said, legal guidance matters at key points, especially on timeliness. The Resources page covers free and low-cost legal help, including New Hampshire Legal Assistance (603-224-3333).

What exactly is the September 2025 ruling?

On September 25, 2025, Administrative Law Judge Zachary Towle denied DCYF's motion to dismiss an administrative appeal of a URC finding, holding that such findings are subject to appeal under RSA 541-A. The case was brought by a Manchester father whose family received a URC finding following a DCYF investigation in 2024.

The ruling is not binding precedent in the way a court decision would be, but it directly contradicts DCYF's longstanding position that URC findings were unreviewable, and provides a legal basis for other families to challenge their own findings.

Will reaching out affect my current or future interactions with DCYF?

We are not DCYF, and reaching out to us has no formal effect on any current or pending DCYF matter. We are private individuals and this is not an official government process.

If you have an active open case with DCYF, you may want to consult with an attorney before taking any formal steps, including filing an appeal.

Who is behind this page?

This page is maintained in connection with Becker v. DCYF et al., Civil No. 1:24-cv-00430-JL-TSM, a federal civil rights case pending in the U.S. District Court for the District of New Hampshire. The plaintiff in that case, Torrence Becker, is a Manchester father who has been pursuing administrative and federal remedies since 2024.
