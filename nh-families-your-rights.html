Your Rights — NH DCYF Families

What the law required DCYF to tell you — and didn't.

Every URC finding DCYF issued was governed by state administrative law. That law required DCYF to include specific findings of fact, conclusions of law, and notice of your right to appeal. None of those requirements were met. This page explains what they were — and why it matters.

RSA 541-A:35

DCYF was legally required to tell you why

When a New Hampshire state agency makes a formal decision that adversely affects you, that decision does not get to just say "we have concerns" and close the file. State law is explicit: every final adverse decision must include specific factual findings and legal conclusions.

RSA 541-A:35 — Final Decisions (Administrative Procedure Act)
"A final decision or order adverse to a party in a contested case shall be in writing… A final decision shall include findings of fact and conclusions of law, separately stated. Findings of fact, if set forth in statutory language, shall be accompanied by a concise and explicit statement of the underlying facts supporting the findings."
New Hampshire Administrative Procedure Act — RSA 541-A:35

This is not a technicality. It is the foundational requirement that gives administrative decisions their legal validity. Without it, a decision cannot meaningfully be contested — and cannot lawfully be used against you.

A URC letter was a final decision that adversely affected you. The September 2025 AAU ruling confirmed this explicitly: URC findings are retained indefinitely, can be used as evidence of a "pattern or course of conduct" in future proceedings, and trigger additional investigative review if multiple reports exist. These are legally cognizable harms. They are the hallmarks of an adverse final decision.

That means the requirements of RSA 541-A:35 applied to every URC letter DCYF ever issued. Not some of them. All of them.

What your letter actually contained

A generic acknowledgment that a report was received. A boilerplate statement that DCYF had "reason to believe" something occurred. Parenting recommendations that were identical across cases. No case-specific facts. No legal analysis. No explanation of what evidence led to the conclusion.

This is not a case of findings being inadequate or insufficiently explained. The required statutory elements are completely absent.

Why does this matter? Because without findings of fact and conclusions of law, you have no meaningful way to challenge the decision. You cannot argue against facts that were never stated. You cannot point to legal error in analysis that was never performed. You are left trying to appeal a shadow — which is precisely what made these findings so difficult to contest.

What Was Missing

Four things every URC letter was required to include

Under RSA 541-A:35 and fundamental due process principles, DCYF's determination letter was required to contain each of the following. None did.

Requirement 1

Specific findings of fact

Not a boilerplate clause. A specific written explanation of what DCYF found to have occurred — what acts, omissions, or circumstances formed the basis for the conclusion. Your letter had none.

Requirement 2

Conclusions of law

An explanation of how the identified facts satisfied the applicable legal standard — in this case, the "probable cause" threshold under RSA 169-C:3, XXIX. Your letter had none.

Requirement 3

The underlying facts supporting those findings

Even where statutory language is used, RSA 541-A:35 requires a "concise and explicit statement" of the underlying facts. A finding cannot lawfully rest on conclusory language alone. Your letter had none.

Requirement 4

Notice of your right to appeal

Due process requires that when government action adversely affects you, you must be told what rights you have to contest it. DCYF's letters provided no such notice — and DCYF actively took the position that no appeal existed.

Not just your letter — every URC letter

DCYF's official form for URC findings — Form 1213 — was used for every family. It contained no space for findings of fact, no space for conclusions of law, and no notice of appeal rights. The agency's own internal policy documents confirm these omissions were built into the process from the start. This was not an oversight in your case. It was how the system was designed.

Notably, the 2026 version of Form 1213 — issued after the September 2025 AAU ruling — still contains no notice of appeal rights.

The Notice Problem

You were never told you could appeal — that was the point.

Administrative agencies are required to inform the people they act against of their rights. When DCYF issued your URC letter without any mention of appeal rights, it was not a clerical oversight. DCYF's institutional position — maintained through litigation — was that URC findings were not appealable at all.

This position is now known to have been legally incorrect. But for years, families were told — directly or by silence — that there was nothing to challenge. Families who tried to push back found no process available to them.

What New Hampshire Administrative Law Requires
"An agency issuing an adverse decision must provide notice of the right to appeal and the applicable procedures."
N.H. Admin. Rule He-C 201 et seq. — DHHS Administrative Appeals Procedures

This requirement exists precisely so that people know their options. When the requirement is absent and the agency simultaneously denies that any appeal exists, the effect — whether intended or not — is to eliminate access to review entirely.

The September 2025 ruling in Appeal of Becker directly contradicts DCYF's longstanding position. ALJ Zachary Towle found that URC findings are subject to administrative appeal under RSA 126-A:5 and 541-A. DCYF's motion to dismiss — which argued that the AAU lacked jurisdiction to hear these appeals at all — was denied.

That ruling means families who were told there was nothing to appeal were misled. The question now being litigated is whether those families' appeal rights were tolled — legally preserved — because of that misrepresentation.

DCYF knew appeals existed — and said nothing

DCYF's own internal policies include a specific procedure for what happens when a URC finding is "overturned" by the AAU — proving the agency understood that administrative review of these findings was possible. Yet those same policies never required that families be told of their right to appeal. The 2026 version of the official URC form, issued after the September 2025 ruling, still contains no appeal notice.

The Probable Cause Paradox

A legal contradiction embedded in every URC finding

Beyond the procedural violations, there is a deeper structural problem with how many URC findings were issued. The legal standard DCYF must meet to issue a URC finding — "probable cause" to believe abuse or neglect occurred — triggers a mandatory legal obligation that DCYF routinely failed to fulfill.

RSA 169-C:34, VII — DCYF's Mandatory Duty
"If the child's parents refuse to allow a social worker or state employee on their premises… and the department has probable cause to believe that the child has been abused or neglected, the department shall seek a court order to enter the premises."
RSA 169-C:34, VII (emphasis added — "shall" is mandatory)

The word "shall" in New Hampshire law is not permissive. It is mandatory. If probable cause existed and entry was refused, DCYF was legally required to seek a court order. This is not a policy choice — it is a statutory command.

If DCYF refused entry to your home was refused during the investigation, this creates the following sequence:

What the statute requires — step by step
✓
You refused DCYF entry to your home during the assessment.
✓
DCYF issued a URC finding — which by statutory definition requires a determination of "probable cause to believe the child was abused or neglected." (RSA 169-C:3, XXIX)
✗
DCYF did not seek a court order for entry — despite the statutory mandate in RSA 169-C:34, VII.
→
This creates a legal contradiction that cannot be resolved. Either probable cause existed or it did not.
If probable cause did NOT exist

Then the URC finding is factually false. The statutory definition of a URC finding requires probable cause. A finding issued without it has no valid legal basis and must be vacated.

If probable cause DID exist

Then DCYF violated RSA 169-C:34, VII by failing to seek the mandatory court order. The assessment was conducted in violation of state law, and any finding resulting from that assessment is tainted by the procedural violation.

There is no third option. Either the finding lacks a valid factual predicate, or the investigation that produced it was conducted contrary to the statute. In both scenarios, the finding cannot stand as issued.

This argument has been raised in active administrative appeals before the AAU and is a central ground in the pending federal civil rights litigation.

Agency's own admission

DCYF's own policy acknowledges that URC findings require probable cause. Yet when asked directly, the agency's Deputy Chief Counsel confirmed in writing that DCYF does not track whether it ever seeks court orders after parents refuse entry — as required by RSA 169-C:34(VII). Two separate DHHS legal offices, six months apart, gave the same answer: no records exist. The absence of any oversight mechanism is itself evidence that this statutory requirement has never been enforced.

The Legal Foundation

How these rights were established — step by step

The legal landscape governing URC findings has shifted significantly since 2024. Here is the sequence of events that produced the current legal framework.

December 2017
Policy Directive 17-72 — Form 1213 issued
DCYF issues the official template for URC findings — Form 1213 — under Policy Directive 17-72. The form contains no space for findings of fact, no conclusions of law, and no notice of appeal rights. Signed by the DCYF Director and managed by the Policy Administrator, this template will be used for every URC finding issued over the next eight years.
November 2024
First administrative appeal of a URC finding filed
A Manchester father — the petitioner in Becker v. DCYF — filed an administrative appeal of a URC finding before the DHHS Administrative Appeals Unit. This was the first appeal of its kind to reach the merits stage. DCYF moved to dismiss, arguing the AAU lacked jurisdiction.
September 15, 2025
DCYF files motion to dismiss — and its briefing
DCYF argued that URC findings were not "final decisions" subject to appeal, that individuals had no legally protected interest in challenging them, and that the AAU lacked authority to grant any relief. The petitioner filed an objection the same day.
September 25, 2025
ALJ Towle denies DCYF's motion to dismiss
Administrative Law Judge Zachary Towle issued the Order on Motion to Dismiss in AAU Docket 2025-1149. The ruling found that URC findings are retained indefinitely, can be used as evidence of a pattern of conduct in future proceedings, and therefore constitute adverse final decisions subject to administrative appeal under RSA 126-A:5 and 541-A. DCYF's motion was denied.
October 22, 2025
Second administrative appeal filed — 2023 finding
Building on the September 25 ruling, the same petitioner filed a second administrative appeal challenging a 2023 URC finding — Assessment #992540. The appeal argued that the finding violated RSA 541-A:35 (no findings of fact or conclusions of law), was issued in violation of RSA 169-C:34(VII), and should be accepted nunc pro tunc given DCYF's failure to provide appeal notice.
January 2, 2026
Third administrative appeal filed — 2025 finding
A second URC finding — Assessment #1153786, dated November 7, 2025 — was appealed. This appeal raised the additional issue that DCYF issued the URC letter after being specifically informed of the September 25 ruling, and continued to omit appeal notice. The appeal also raised the probable cause paradox and the arbitrary and capricious nature of simultaneously finding "reasonable concerns" while recommending the subject of those concerns as the child's primary protective figure.
Ongoing
Federal civil rights case — U.S. District Court, D.N.H.
A parallel federal civil rights action — Becker v. DCYF et al., Civil No. 1:24-cv-00430-JL-TSM — is pending before the U.S. District Court for the District of New Hampshire, raising constitutional and civil rights claims arising from the same conduct.
February 17, 2026
Second agency admission — no RSA 169-C:34(VII) tracking
The DHHS Legal Unit Coordinator confirms in writing — for the second time, six months after the first — that the agency does not track whether DCYF seeks court orders when parents refuse entry during an investigation. No records exist. This is not a gap in one case file; it reflects an institutional decision never to monitor compliance with a mandatory statutory requirement.
What This Means for You

Possible grounds to challenge your URC finding

Whether any of these grounds apply to your situation depends on the specific facts of your case. But these are the legal arguments that are currently being raised and litigated. If your URC letter was issued without any of the following, you may have grounds to challenge it.

✓

No findings of fact in your letter

If your letter contained no specific explanation of what acts or omissions DCYF found — just boilerplate language about "reason to believe" — then it may violate RSA 541-A:35. Every URC letter DCYF issued used the same standardized form. None contained case-specific findings of fact.

✓

No conclusions of law in your letter

If your letter provided no legal analysis explaining how the identified facts satisfied the "probable cause" standard under RSA 169-C:3, XXIX, the letter is missing a mandatory element of a valid final decision under RSA 541-A:35.

✓

No notice of appeal rights

If your letter provided no information about your right to appeal — or if you were affirmatively told no appeal was available — the notice requirement was violated. This is also relevant to the "good cause" argument for accepting a late-filed appeal: you were not told a deadline existed because you were told no process existed.

✓

You refused DCYF entry to your home

If DCYF was denied access to your home during the assessment and did not seek a court order, yet still issued a URC finding, the probable cause paradox may apply. Either the finding lacks a valid factual basis or the assessment violated RSA 169-C:34, VII. This is a separate and independent ground for challenging the finding.

✓

The finding contradicts itself internally

If your letter simultaneously identified you as a "concern" while also identifying you as the child's primary safety resource or recommending you maintain guardianship, this internal contradiction may render the finding arbitrary and capricious — a recognized ground for administrative reversal under New Hampshire law.

Time Limits & Late Filings

It may not be too late — even if your letter is years old

Administrative appeals normally must be filed within a set time period after the decision is issued. Under New Hampshire administrative rules, the standard deadline to appeal a DHHS decision is 30 days.

That deadline passed for nearly every family that received a URC finding — because they were never told an appeal was available. DCYF maintained, until the September 2025 ruling, that URC findings were not subject to appeal at all.

The question of whether those missed deadlines are fatal to a late appeal is unresolved. It is being actively litigated and has not been decided by the AAU, the Superior Court, or any appellate body. Two legal arguments are being made for why late appeals should still be accepted — but neither has been won yet:

Argument 1 — Estoppel / Tolling by Agency Misrepresentation

The agency cannot benefit from its own misrepresentation

DCYF affirmatively represented that URC findings were not appealable. Families relied on that representation. When an agency causes a delay in filing through its own conduct, it cannot then invoke the deadline as a bar. The doctrine of equitable tolling preserves appeal rights in precisely these circumstances.

Argument 2 — Nunc Pro Tunc / Good Cause

The appeal should be accepted as though timely filed

The doctrine of nunc pro tunc — "now for then" — permits an administrative body to accept a late filing as though it had been timely filed when extraordinary circumstances prevented timely action through no fault of the appellant. The combination of no appeal notice, no deadline stated, and DCYF's explicit denial of any appeal right satisfies this standard.

These arguments have not yet been decided

Neither the equitable tolling argument nor the nunc pro tunc argument has been resolved by a ruling. They are live issues in ongoing proceedings. A family that files a late appeal should understand going in that DCYF will contest timeliness, and that the outcome of that contest is genuinely uncertain — it is not a settled question with a known answer.

What is certain is that the September 2025 ruling establishing that URC findings are appealable at all is the legal foundation for everything else. Without it, none of these arguments could be made.

Common Questions

Questions about the legal arguments

What does "findings of fact and conclusions of law" actually mean in plain language?

"Findings of fact" means a written statement of what the agency determined to have actually happened — the specific events, behaviors, or circumstances that led to the decision. In a DCYF case, this would mean: what was reported, what was observed, what interviews occurred, and what specific information led DCYF to conclude probable cause existed.

"Conclusions of law" means a statement of how those facts satisfy the applicable legal standard. In a URC case: why the identified facts constitute "probable cause" under RSA 169-C:3, XXIX — meaning why they would lead a reasonable person to believe abuse or neglect occurred.

The URC letters DCYF sent contained neither. They said, in effect: "We received a report. We have reason to believe something happened. The case is closed." That is not a findings-and-conclusions analysis — it is a conclusion without support.

Why does it matter that the findings are missing? The case was closed — won't it just stay in the file?

URC findings are retained indefinitely under RSA 169-C:35-a, III. More importantly, they can be introduced in future proceedings as evidence of a "pattern or course of conduct" under RSA 169-C:12. If DCYF investigates your family again, a prior URC finding will be in your record — and if there are two or more URC or unfounded reports involving your family, it triggers mandatory administrative review under RSA 169-C:34, II(b).

The finding does not simply sit in a file. It accumulates. A finding that could not be challenged when it was issued can become evidence at a future hearing where the stakes may be significantly higher.

Can I demand that DCYF provide findings of fact even now?

You can request them. Whether DCYF is obligated to provide them retroactively is part of what is being litigated. The argument being raised in current appeals is that a decision issued without the required findings is invalid — not merely incomplete — and should be vacated and expunged rather than remanded for DCYF to reconstruct findings years later.

The argument against remand is a strong one: you cannot fairly challenge findings that are being reconstructed from memory years after the investigation closed. Due process requires findings to be made contemporaneously, not retrofitted after the fact.

Does the probable cause argument apply if I let DCYF into my home?

No. The probable cause paradox under RSA 169-C:34, VII specifically applies when DCYF was refused entry and did not seek a court order. If you allowed DCYF access during the investigation, this particular argument does not apply — though the other grounds (missing findings of fact, missing conclusions of law, no appeal notice) may still be available.

Is the September 2025 ruling binding on future cases?

The ALJ ruling is not binding precedent in the same way that a New Hampshire Supreme Court decision would be. Administrative law judge decisions do not formally bind future proceedings. However, the ruling directly contradicts the legal position DCYF had asserted, provides a reasoned basis for other families to challenge their findings, and — critically — it was the first ruling of its kind. Its persuasive weight is substantial even if it is not technically binding.

The federal civil rights case and any eventual AAU final decisions on the merits will produce additional precedent on these questions.

Was my case the only one where these things were missing?

No. DCYF used the same standardized form — Form 1213, issued under Policy Directive 17-72 — for every URC finding between 2017 and 2026. That form had no place for findings of fact, no conclusions of law, and no notice of appeal rights. Thousands of families received identical letters with identical omissions.

The problem is not your case. It is the system itself. DCYF's own policy documents confirm these elements were never included in the official template — even after the September 2025 AAU ruling established that URC findings are appealable.

What is the difference between the administrative appeal and the federal lawsuit?

The administrative appeals before the DHHS Administrative Appeals Unit are state administrative proceedings. They are decided by an Administrative Law Judge and can result in the finding being vacated and expunged from DCYF's records. The process is governed by state administrative law (RSA 541-A and DHHS rules). A step-by-step overview of how to file is on the Resources page.

The federal civil rights case — Becker v. DCYF et al., Civil No. 1:24-cv-00430-JL-TSM — is a separate lawsuit in U.S. District Court. It raises constitutional and federal statutory claims (including 42 U.S.C. § 1983) arising from the same conduct. The two proceedings are parallel and complementary, not mutually exclusive.
