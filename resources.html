Free & Low-Cost Legal Help

Finding a lawyer for this is genuinely difficult.

Administrative appeals of DCYF findings sit in a narrow area of law — not quite family court, not quite standard administrative law — and most general practitioners won't take them. Legal aid organizations are the most realistic starting point for most families, but they are stretched thin and eligibility-gated. This list is honest about what's actually available, not what should be available.

If you cannot find an attorney, the process can be done without one. But the later steps — especially if DCYF contests timeliness or files motions — become significantly harder to navigate alone. Keep trying.

Free civil legal services

New Hampshire Legal Assistance

NHLA provides free civil legal help to low-income New Hampshire residents. Family law, administrative appeals, and DCYF-related matters are within their scope. Call or use the online intake to find out if you qualify.

603-224-3333 nhla.org

A note on eligibility and capacity

Free legal aid programs use income guidelines to determine eligibility, and their capacity is limited. If you've been told you don't qualify, or been turned away, try the other organizations on this list — eligibility rules and availability vary. LARC can help map all your options at once.

If you've exhausted the legal aid options and can't afford a private attorney, document that you tried. It matters if your case ever reaches a point where the question of whether you had meaningful access to counsel becomes relevant.

If you have an active open case with DCYF, speak with an attorney before taking any formal steps on a prior finding.

The Appeal Process

How to file an administrative appeal

Administrative appeals of DCYF findings go to the DHHS Administrative Appeals Unit (AAU). The September 2025 ALJ ruling confirmed that URC findings are subject to appeal under RSA 541-A. Here is what that process looks like.

1

Gather your documentation

Locate your original URC finding letter. If you no longer have it, you need to request your case file from DCYF — this is its own process with specific requirements. See the Requesting Your Records section below for the correct procedure, required form, and what to ask for.

2

Write your petition for appeal

Your appeal is initiated by filing a written petition with the DHHS Administrative Appeals Unit. The petition should identify: who you are, the finding you are appealing, the date of the finding, and a brief statement of why you believe it should be reviewed. You do not need to present your full legal argument at this stage.

3

File with the Administrative Appeals Unit

Submit your petition to the DHHS Administrative Appeals Unit in writing. The AAU can be reached at: DHHS Administrative Appeals Unit, 129 Pleasant Street, Concord, NH 03301. Send by certified mail and keep your receipt. Address your petition to the attention of the AAU Director.

4

Be aware of the timeliness issue if your finding is older

If your finding is from 2021, 2022, or earlier, DCYF may argue the appeal is untimely. There is active litigation over whether the normal time limits apply to families who were told no appeal right existed — this question has not been finally resolved. This is the point in the process where legal guidance matters most. An attorney familiar with NH administrative law can advise you on whether and how to address timeliness given your specific circumstances.

5

If DCYF moves to dismiss, you have the right to respond

DCYF has previously moved to dismiss appeals of URC findings on jurisdictional grounds. If they do so in your case, you have the right to file a written response before the AAU rules. The September 2025 ruling in the Becker case addressed this exact argument and is a public record. What that ruling means for your specific situation is a question for an attorney.

6

If the AAU rules against you, the process continues

An adverse AAU ruling can be appealed to the NH Superior Court under RSA 541-A:17. From there, appeals proceed to the NH Supreme Court. Separately, if your situation involves constitutional violations — denial of due process, unlawful search, or similar claims — those may support a federal civil rights action under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, which is a distinct proceeding from the administrative appeal. Whether any of those federal theories apply to your facts is a question that requires an attorney; the administrative appeal and any potential federal claims are different roads that don't automatically travel together.

Getting Your Case File

How to request your records from DCYF

Child welfare records are confidential under RSA 170-G:8-a, which means the standard Right-to-Know process does not apply. You have to follow a separate internal procedure. Using the wrong process is the most common reason requests stall for months.

For your family's case file

Use DCYF's internal request process

Case files are confidential records. RSA 91-A (Right-to-Know) does not apply. You must use the DCYF records request form with ID verification.

For general agency records

Use the Right-to-Know law (RSA 91-A)

Policies, training materials, statistics — records that don't identify individual families — can be requested via RSA 91-A. Send to DHHS Legal at [email protected]. Verify this address before sending.

1

Get and complete the official form

DCYF requires their specific records request form (sometimes called Form 2765). Email the DCYF district office that handled your case and ask for it, or check the DCYF website under "Records & Reports." They will not process a request without it, even a detailed letter. Include a clear copy of your ID (front and back), and if requesting records for a child, documentation of your parental relationship.

2

Be specific about what you want

Do not write "my file." List everything explicitly so the agency cannot later claim your request was too broad — and ask specifically for audio and video recordings, which are often omitted if not named.

Sample request language

I am requesting the complete case file for my children: [Child full name, date of birth].

This includes but is not limited to:

  • All case notes and contact logs
  • All reports and assessments
  • All correspondence (emails, letters, memos)
  • All audio and video recordings of any interviews, meetings, or phone calls
  • All BRIDGES system data related to my family
  • All safety plans, incident reports, and notices of decision
3

Wait — and follow up at 60 days

Processing generally takes up to 60 days. You will not receive status updates unless you ask. If you haven't heard anything after 60 days, send a written follow-up requesting a status update. Keep a copy of everything you send.

4

Review carefully — then follow up on anything missing

When records arrive, check them against the contact logs. If a log entry references a recording or document that wasn't included, write back immediately and ask for it specifically in writing.

Sample follow-up language

Subject: Follow-up on records request – [Your Name]

I received the records from my request on [date]. However, the following items referenced in the contact logs were not included:

  • Audio recording of phone call on [date] with [worker name]
  • [Any other missing items]

Please confirm whether these recordings exist. If they do, please provide them. If they do not exist or cannot be provided, please explain in writing why they are unavailable.

Common mistakes

Mistake What to do instead
Using RSA 91-A to request your case file Use DCYF's internal process. Right-to-Know does not apply to confidential child welfare records.
Not asking specifically for audio recordings Be explicit. Ask for "all audio and video recordings" in writing. They are frequently omitted if not named.
Assuming they sent everything Cross-reference what you receive against the contact logs. Follow up in writing on anything missing.
Not keeping copies of your request and correspondence Save every email, form, and response. You may need them later.
If you encounter problems

No response after 60 days — Follow up in writing to the person who acknowledged your request. If no response, escalate to a supervisor in writing.

Denial of access — Request a written explanation citing the specific statutory exemption they are relying on. You can file a complaint with the DHHS Ombudsman ([email protected] — verify before sending). If the denial involves a finding, you may have appeal rights through the AAU.

Missing audio recordings — Request a written explanation. If the agency claims recordings don't exist, ask in writing for documentation of their records retention policy and any destruction logs.

Statutory Framework

The laws that govern your case

These are the New Hampshire statutes most directly relevant to challenging a URC finding. The full text of each is available through the NH General Court's online database, linked below.

RSA 541-A:35

Required contents of a final decision

Every final adverse decision must include findings of fact and conclusions of law, separately stated. This is the provision that URC letters systematically violated — they contained neither.

RSA 541-A:35 — Full text
RSA 541-A:31

Right to appeal a contested case decision

Any party to a contested case adversely affected by a final decision has the right to appeal. This is the statutory basis for administrative review of DCYF findings — the right DCYF told families didn't exist.

RSA 541-A:31 — Full text
RSA 170-G:8-d

Access to DCYF case records

Authorizes individuals to request records related to DCYF investigations involving themselves or their family. This is how you obtain documentation of your own finding if your letter is lost or incomplete.

RSA 170-G:8-d — Full text
RSA 169-C:3

Definitions — Child Protection Act

Defines key terms used in DCYF investigations, including "founded," "unfounded," and the classification categories that determine how a finding is recorded and what consequences follow.

RSA 169-C:3 — Full text
RSA 541-A:17

Judicial review of AAU decisions

If the Administrative Appeals Unit rules against you, this statute provides the pathway for appealing that decision to the New Hampshire Superior Court.

RSA 541-A:17 — Full text
